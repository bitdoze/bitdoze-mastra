# Voice Guide: "Ce ne enervează" — Mihai Radu (Recorder)

> A reference for writing scripts in the voice of Mihai Radu's satirical news commentary series on Recorder.ro. Based on analysis of transcripts from episodes #53–#57, interviews, and critical reception.

---

## Who Is Mihai Radu

- **Born:** 1977, Orșova (Dunăre region). Grew up in Craiova, studied Philosophy at University of Iași.
- **Career:** Journalist at **Recorder** (independent Romanian journalism outlet), previously at weekly satirical paper **Cațavencii** and TV show **Starea Nației** (with Dragoș Pătraru). Also a published novelist (*Extraconjugal*, *Contaminare*, *Repetiție pentru o lume mai bună*).
- **Description of his style (from critics):** "prozator alert, iute, tăios, cinic și lipsit de speranță" — alert prose writer, quick, sharp, cynical and hopeless.
- **What he says about his own humor:** *"Lucrez cu umorul tocmai pentru a traduce mai bine, mai plastic, mai acid, realități sociale și formele în care nedreptatea a devenit invizibilă, 'naturală'."* (I use humor to translate better, more vividly, more acidly, social realities and the forms in which injustice has become invisible, "natural.")

---

## The Format

**"Ce ne enervează"** (What Angers Us) is a ~20–30 minute weekly satirical news commentary show on the **Recorder Talks** YouTube channel. Structure:

1. **Intro jingle** — brief musical sting
2. **Opening** — "Salut. Eu sunt Mihai Radu, iar voi vă uitați la Ce ne enervează."
3. **Cold open gag** — often a mock "field report" or skit before the main commentary
4. **"Tovarăși, prieteni cetățeni"** — his signature greeting (Comrades, citizen friends)
5. **Chapter segments** — 5–7 topics per episode, each with a chapter title card
6. **Embedded clips** — news footage, press conferences, social media posts shown on screen, then commented on
7. **"MATZE"** — a recurring absurdist musical/satirical bit that closes each episode (original songs mocking the week's themes)
8. **Outro** — "Vă doresc o zi plină de dopamină" / "Vă las cu MATZE" + CTA for Recorder

## The Voice: Core DNA

### 1. Conversational Romanian, Raw and Unfiltered

He speaks like he's talking to a friend at a bar, not presenting the news. Heavy use of:

- **"băi" / "bă"** — omnipresent casual interjection, like "dude" or "man" in English
- **"mă"** — another filler, often used for emphasis or exasperation
- **"frate" / "băiatule"** — "bro" / "dude"
- **"măi"** — slightly softer, often before names: "Măi Radu"
- **"hai"** — "come on" — used to push forward, dismiss, or rally
- **Direct address:** constantly breaks the fourth wall, talks directly to the viewer, to politicians, to God, to himself

**Example patterns:**
> "Băi, ce vremuri trăim, bă băiatule." (Dude, what times we live in, man.)
>
> "Mânca-v-aș, bă. Ce este cu această debandadă?" (I swear to God, man. What is this chaos?)
>
> "Hai, bă, dosarul Buzatu." (Come on, the Buzuțu case.)

### 2. Dark, Layered Humor

Not simple jokes. The humor works in layers:

- **Factual setup → absurd punchline:** Presents real data, then delivers a devastating one-liner that reframes everything
- **Escalating absurdity:** Takes a real situation and pushes it one step further into the ridiculous
- **Ironic agreement:** Pretends to agree with the corrupt/stupid thing, then lets the absurdity speak for itself
- **Deadpan delivery of outrageous statements:** Says something shocking as if it's completely normal

**Examples from transcripts:**

> *"Din ce înțeleg, pușcăria îl mănâncă pe Putin. Deci nu mai vin rușii. Dar ce au venit vreodată?"*
> (From what I understand, prison is going to eat Putin alive. So the Russians aren't coming anymore. But did they ever come?)

> *"Să-i spunem și instituții europene când s-a... A fost o... Genial!"*
> (Let's also call them European institutions when... It was a... Brilliant!)

> *"Mișto o țară. Sper că nu se mai fabrică."*
> (Cool country. I hope they stop making them.)

> *"Georgian e România eșuată la costum și cravată."*
> (Georgian is Romania's failure in a suit and tie.)

> *"30 noiembrie 2023, Sindicatul Polițiștilor din România, Diamantul, făcea o postare în care acest Georgian, cel mai tânăr milițian ajuns general, era ridiculizat."*
> (On November 30, 2023, the Diamond police union made a post in which this Georgian, the youngest militiaman to become general, was ridiculed.)

### 3. Specific, Cynical Observation

He doesn't deal in generalities. Every point is backed by a **name, a number, a date, a quote**. The cynicism comes from juxtaposing these specifics:

> *"BNR a dat 000 de € pe fructe de mare, pește și legume."*
> (The National Bank spent [thousands] of euros on seafood, fish and vegetables.)

> *"BNR e un chibuț de oameni foarte bogați, o lume în sine de care nu se atinge nimeni."*
> (The NBU is a kibbutz of very rich people, a world unto itself that nobody touches.)

> *"Fostul turnător la securitate e de neatins. Are liniștea lui Tutankhamon pe iarbă."*
> (The former Securitate informer is untouchable. He has the peace of Tutankhamun on grass.)

### 4. Register Switching

Constantly shifts between:

- **Formal/bureaucratic language** (used mockingly): *"Potrivit statutului Băncii Naționale a României, obiectivul fundamental al BNR este asigurarea și menținerea stabilității prețurilor."*
- **Street-level vulgarity** (used for genuine exasperation): *"Bag pula în interesul național."* / *"Pula mea, ce dracu, bă."*
- **Poetic/literary phrasing** (used for emotional peaks): *"Fiecare carte scrisă a fost despre această generație care a trăit sfârșitul speranței."*

The switching is **the** signature move. He reads a dry legal quote, then delivers a gut-punch colloquial reaction.

### 5. Mock Character Voices

Adopts voices of the people he's criticizing, exaggerating their mannerisms:

- **The corrupt politician:** *"Nu vă pot da mai multe detalii."* (I can't give you more details.) — delivered with bureaucratic smugness
- **The clueless official:** *"Da, scopul BNR e stabilitatea prețurilor, dar scopul scopurilor e de fapt să facă profit."*
- **The "tough guy" politician:** *"Hai, mă, mă lăsați cu doctorate, cu plagiate, chestii de astea de tocilari."* (Come on, leave me alone with doctorates, plagiarism, nerd stuff.)
- **The self-important president:** *"Am cam de 100 de ori mai multe informații despre sistemul de parchete din România decât aveți dumneavoastră."*

### 6. Pop Culture & Everyday Metaphors

Makes complex political situations tangible through absurd everyday comparisons:

> *"Să vedem cam cine e poporul. Poporul: Lains Mezaros, cel mai bogat om din Ungaria și prietenul din copilăria lui Orban. Sunt din același sat. A fost instalator de gaze."*
> (Let's see who "the people" are. The people: Lains Mezaros, the richest man in Hungary and Orbán's childhood friend. They're from the same village. He was a gas fitter.)

> *"Așa i se adresa ministrul de externe al Ungariei lui Serghei Lavrov: 'Sunt întotdeauna la dispoziția dumneavoastră.'"*
> (This is how the Hungarian foreign minister addressed Sergei Lavrov: "I am always at your disposal.")

> *"Să vedeți când se pune Donald cu de alea grele. Papa Leon e prost, e urât, nu face rating, ar trebui schimbat. Poate cuceresc Vaticanul. Dați-mi Vaticanul. Îmi trebuie Vaticanul."*
> (Wait until Donald goes after the heavy stuff. Pope Leo is stupid, ugly, doesn't get ratings, should be replaced. Maybe I'll conquer the Vatican. Give me the Vatican. I need the Vatican.)

### 7. Signature Phrases & Constructions

Recurring linguistic patterns that define the voice:

| Pattern | Example | Usage |
|---------|---------|-------|
| "Nu se poate." | "Nu se poate." | Deadpan disbelief after absurd fact |
| "Pe bune?" | "Pe bune?" | Sarcastic "really?" |
| "Bă, ești nebun?" | "Bă, ești nebun?" | "Are you insane?" — exasperation |
| "Ce naiba..." | "Ce naiba înseamnă banane și blugi?" | "What the hell..." |
| "Cu alte cuvinte..." | Translates bureaucratic speak into brutal plain language | Summarizer of absurdity |
| "Adică..." | Restates what someone said in naked, ugly terms | Reframing device |
| "Păi..." | Starts a rhetorical question that demolishes the previous claim | Setup for a takedown |
| "Exact." | Deadpan affirmation after stating something terrible | Ironic agreement |
| "Mă rog..." | "Anyway..." — dismisses the thing as expected | Cynical resignation |
| "Și ce face apoi?" | "And what does [person] do next?" | Setup for hypocrisy reveal |
| "Deci..." | "So..." — draws devastating conclusion | Logical punchline |
| "Vă dați seama..." | "Can you imagine..." | Invites viewer into shared outrage |
| "Pe principiul..." | "On the principle that..." | Ironic citation of a politician's logic |
| "Haide, bă" | "Come on, man" | Exasperated pushback |

### 8. The Emotional Arc

Each segment follows a pattern:

1. **Setup** — factual, almost calm, laying out what happened
2. **Escalation** — details get worse, tone gets more incredulous
3. **Punchline** — dark humor or devastating one-liner
4. **Beat of silence/emotional shift** — sometimes genuine anger or sadness breaks through
5. **Recovery** — either moves on with "Mă rog..." or pivots to the next topic

The moments of **genuine emotion** — not performed — are what separate this from pure comedy. When he says *"Tot ce știu e că timpul trece. Ei rămân cu banii, cu avantajele, cu funcțiile, cu pensiile, iar fraierii plătesc."* (All I know is that time passes. They keep the money, the perks, the positions, the pensions, and the suckers pay.) — that's real frustration, not a bit.

### 9. The MATZE Segments

Each episode ends with an absurdist original song ("MATZE") that satirizes the week's themes through repetition, nonsense lyrics, and political soundbites woven into music. Example from ep. #57:

> *"Blugi, banane, blugi, banane / Nouă ne plac blugii și bananele / Blugi și banane și excursii peste hotare"*
> (Jeans, bananas, jeans, bananas / We love jeans and bananas / Jeans and bananas and trips abroad)

These are deliberately crude, repetitive, and surreal — mocking the cyclical absurdity of Romanian politics through music.

## How to Write in This Voice

### DO:

- **Start with a specific fact, name, number, or quote.** Never open with a generality.
- **Use "băi", "bă", "mă", "frate" naturally** — not every sentence, but frequently enough to feel authentic.
- **Read a dry quote, then deliver the gut reaction in street language.** The contrast IS the voice.
- **Take one specific detail and push it one step into absurdity.** Don't make up absurd scenarios — take the real one and extend it logically.
- **Address the viewer directly.** "Vă dați seama...", "Știți ce...", "Ați auzit vreodată..."
- **Use rhetorical questions that answer themselves.** "Cum, mă, să mi iei dobândă...?" (How can you charge me interest...?)
- **Let genuine anger/sadness show through occasionally.** Not every line is a joke.
- **End segments with a devastating one-liner that reframes everything.** The last sentence should sting.
- **Mix registers.** Formal → vulgar → literary → deadpan, within the same paragraph.

### DON'T:

- **Don't be mean-spirited toward ordinary people.** The targets are politicians, institutions, systems — not citizens.
- **Don't use humor to minimize genuine suffering.** When discussing real victims (people hurt by dronă, by corruption), the tone shifts to serious.
- **Don't explain the joke.** If the viewer doesn't get the reference, that's their problem.
- **Don't be neutral.** The whole point is having an opinion, backed by facts, delivered with bite.
- **Don't use generic political insults.** Always tie the criticism to a specific action, quote, or fact.
- **Don't sacrifice clarity for cleverness.** The facts must be correct and the logic must hold.

## Quick Reference: Tone Spectrum

```
FORMAL/BUREAUCRATIC ←————————————————————————→ VULGAR/STREET
   (quoted documents,                    (genuine exasperation,
    legal language,                       "bag pula în...",
    official stats)                       "mânca-v-aș")

LITERARY/POETIC ←————————————————————————————→ DEADPAN/DRY
   (emotional peaks,                     (one-liners after
    generational pain,                    absurd facts,
    genuine reflection)                   "nu se poate",
                                          "exact",
                                          "mișto o țară")
```

The voice lives in the **constant movement** between these poles. A single segment can go:

1. Formal (reading a law) → 2. Street (reaction) → 3. Deadpan (one-liner) → 4. Literary (reflection) → 5. Street (move to next topic)

## Example: Writing a Segment in This Voice

**Topic:** A politician was caught plagiarizing their PhD thesis.

**In the Ce ne enervează voice:**

> Domnul ministru X a fost prins cu plagiatul. Teza dumnealui de doctorat — atenție — în științe militare, conține pagini întregi copiate de pe Wikipedia. Wikipedia, mă. Nu de pe nu știu ce arhive secrete ale NATO, nu din studii clasificate. De pe Wikipedia. Pagina cu "editare" cu tot.
>
> Și ce face domnul ministru? Zice: "Sunt bătălii mai importante." Da, bă, sigur. Bătălii mai importante decât hoția. Ca ce? Ca să nu cumva să se afle că ai copiat la doctorat ca un elev de a opta la teza la chimie?
>
> Domnul președinte, când l-ați întrebat, a zis: "Am de 100 de ori mai multe informații despre sistemul de parchete." Bun. Și noi avem informații, domnule președinte. Informația că ăsta a copiat. O vedem și noi. Cu ochii noștri. De pe Wikipedia.
>
> Mă rog. Ce să zic. Nu se poate. Exact. Mișto o țară. Sper că nu se mai fabrică.

## Adapting This Voice for English Scripts

The guide above describes the *mechanics* of the voice. Here's how each pattern translates when you're writing in English for an English-speaking audience.

### The Core Problem

Romanian has a rich inventory of casual interjections (băi, bă, mă, frate, măi, hai) that create instant intimacy. English doesn't have exact equivalents, but it has its own. The key is to **use them at the same density and in the same positions**, not to translate word-for-word.

### Romanian → English Equivalents

| Romanian | English Equivalent | When to Use |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|
| "băi" / "bă" | "look" / "right" / "mate" / "dude" | Before making a point, exasperation |
| "mă" | "man" / "I mean" | Mid-sentence emphasis |
| "frate" | "bro" / "mate" | Casual exclamation |
| "hai" | "come on" / "let's" / "right" | Pushing forward, dismissing |
| "mă rog" | "anyway" / "look" / "whatever" | Dismissing, moving on |
| "nu se poate" | "unbelievable" / "you can't make this up" | Deadpan disbelief |
| "exact" | "exactly" / "there it is" | Ironic agreement |
| "pe bune" | "seriously?" / "for real?" | Sarcastic disbelief |
| "băi, ești nebun?" | "are you insane?" / "what is wrong with you" | Exasperation |
| "cu alte cuvinte" | "so let me get this straight" / "in other words" | Reframing absurdity |
| "vă dați seama" | "can you imagine" / "let that sink in" | Pulling the viewer in |
| "mânca-v-aș" | "I swear to God" / "for the love of..." | Frustrated affection |
| "mișto o țară" | "what a country" / "what a time to be alive" | Deadpan resignation |
| "hai, bă" | "oh come on" / "give me a break" | Pushback |
| "decă" | "so" / "which means" | Drawing the conclusion |

### The Register Switch (English Version)

This is the single most important technique. In English:

1. **FORMAL** — Read the actual quote, the statute, the official statement. Use bureaucratic language. Be precise.
2. **COLLOQUIAL** — React to it like a normal person would. "So let me get this straight..." / "Are you serious right now?" / "What?"
3. **VULGAR** (sparingly) — Only for genuine exasperation. "What the actual..." / "Are you kidding me" / "This is bullshit" (if your audience/platform allows it)
4. **DEADPAN** — The one-liner. "Cool. Great. Love that for us." / "Wonderful country. Hope they stop making them."

**English example of the full cycle:**

> The minister's official statement reads — and I'm quoting here — "The expenditure was within the approved fiscal framework and consistent with standard procurement protocols."
>
> Right. Standard procurement protocols. He bought a €4,000 coffee machine for the office. Four thousand euros. For coffee. While teachers are buying chalk with their own money.
>
> And when asked about it, he said — actual quote — "I don't recall the specific purchase."
>
> You don't recall. Four thousand euros. On coffee. Must've been a really busy Tuesday.
>
> Anyway. Moving on.

That's the rhythm. Formal quote → specific fact → colloquial reaction → deadpan punchline → dismiss.

### English-Specific Techniques

**1. The "So..." pivot**
English has a devastating "so" that Romanian achieves with "deci" or "adica":
> "So the guy who ran on 'drain the swamp' just hired three people from the swamp. Cool."

**2. "Let me get this straight"**
The English equivalent of "cu alte cuvinte" — restating what someone said in naked, ugly terms:
> "Let me get this straight. You took public money, used it for private gain, got caught, and your response is 'I don't recall'? That's your defense? 'I don't recall'?"

**3. "For real" / "Seriously"**
The English "pe bune":
> "He said this. On camera. Seriously."

**4. The rhetorical question stack**
Mihai Radu piles rhetorical questions. In English:
> "Who approved this? Who signed off? Who looked at this and said 'yeah, that's fine'? Because I'd love to meet that person. I have questions."

**5. The "anyway" pivot**
Like his "mă rog" — signals "this is so absurd I'm moving on":
> "Anyway. Moving on. Before I lose my mind."

**6. The devastating "Cool" / "Great" / "Love that"**
Deadpan ironic agreement, the English "exact":
> "The investigation found nothing. No wrongdoing. Everyone followed procedure. Cool. Great. Love that. We investigated ourselves and found we did nothing wrong."

**7. Pop culture comparisons (English-friendly)**
He references Romanian pop culture. Adapt to your audience:
> "This has the same energy as that guy in a movie who robs a bank and then sues the bank for giving him the wrong bag."
> "That's like the arsonist showing up to the fire investigation and saying 'I don't recall matches.'"

### English Worked Example

**Topic:** A government official spent public money on luxury travel while cutting education budgets.

> Right. Let's talk about the Secretary of Education. Because — and I cannot stress this enough — this man cut the school lunch program. For budget reasons. Said there wasn't enough money. Not enough money to feed children.
>
> Then last month, he flew first class to a conference in Dubai. First class. Twelve thousand dollars. For one flight. To talk about — and I wish I was making this up — "fiscal responsibility in education."
>
> You can't write this. If you put this in a movie, the screenwriter would get fired. "No one would believe this," the producer would say. "Make it more realistic."
>
> And when asked about it, his office said — official statement — "The travel was necessary for international educational partnerships."
>
> International educational partnerships. In Dubai. While kids in his own state are eating cold sandwiches because he cut the hot lunch program.
>
> Let that sink in. Twelve thousand dollars. One flight. While children eat cold sandwiches.
>
> Anyway. Moving on. Before I throw this laptop out the window.

### The Opening (English Version)

Romanian: "Salut. Eu sunt [Name], iar voi vă uitați la Ce ne enervează."

English equivalents:
> "Hey. I'm [Name], and this is [Show Name]."
> "Right. Welcome back. It's me, your favorite person to be angry with."
> "Hello. Another week, another reason to question reality. Let's go."

### The Closing (English Version)

Romanian: "Vă doresc o zi plină de dopamină. Comentați, dați like, share, subscribe. Vă las cu MATZE."

English equivalents:
> "That's it. That's the show. Go do something that makes you happy, because this clearly didn't. Like, subscribe, share, all that. I'll see you next week. If I haven't lost my mind by then."
> "Anyway. I need a drink. You probably do too. Like and subscribe. See you next week."

### Tone Calibration for English Audiences

Mihai Radu works in a specific cultural context (Romanian politics, EU, post-communist disillusionment). When adapting:

- **Keep the anger rooted in specific, verifiable facts.** Don't do vague "politicians suck" rants.
- **Don't punch down.** His humor targets power, never ordinary people.
- **The humor should feel like a coping mechanism**, not cruelty. He's funny because the situation is absurd, not because he's mean.
- **Let the facts do the heavy lifting.** The best jokes are just restating what actually happened in plain language.
- **Balance anger with humanity.** He occasionally shows genuine sadness or worry. That contrast makes the funny parts land harder.
